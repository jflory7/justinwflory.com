The Kite Runner by Khaled Hosseini

Themes (and Things) to Ponder

Redemption

  • Pg. 2, 310: “There is a way to be good again.” - Rahim Khan

  • Pg. 29: Regards to Amir making up stories; he would give old toys and items to Hassan to help ameliorate his guilt

  • Pg. 46-47: Hassan's cleft lip; Baba is trying to redeem himself to Hassan by fixing his lip in ways that he is limited to help

  • Pg. 65: Amir and Hassan in kite fighting; to redeem himself to Baba for “killing his mother”

  • Pg. 105: Baba's forgiveness over Hassan “stealing” his watch and money; Hassan is forgiven by Baba, and Amir is saved from losing Baba's love for setting him up

  • Pg. 164: Soraya redeems herself to Amir by confessing to her past and telling Amir; Amir has the opportunity to confess his past and redeem himself, but chooses not

  • Pg. 168: Baba has an opportunity for redemption when he gives Amir up for marriage, calling him his only son, which is not true

  • Pg. 171: Under the veil; Amir has the opportunity to redeem himself honestly to Soraya in a moment of honesty, but he is still hiding his secret from her even entering the marriage

  • Pg. 210-211: Hassan's mother redeems herself by coming back and taking care of Sohrab.

  • Pg. 237: Farid and Amir; Farid believes Amir to be manipulating Afghanistan, but Amir justifies his cause and redeems himself in the eyes of Farid

  • Pg. 290-291:

  • Pg. 302: Baba's redemption; why he was a good Samaritan to compensate for his sin

  • Pg. 361: Amir is redeeming himself for Baba; Baba would never stand by to have Ali and Hassan persecuted, and Amir is standing up for himself and Sohrab by protecting him from racism

  • Pg. 371: Amir's full redemption comes full circle; Sohrab smiles, and Amir begins to feel that his original sin with Hassan is finally beginning to be redeemed

Forgiveness

  • Pg. 105: Baba's forgiveness over Hassan “stealing” his watch and money; Hassan is forgiven by Baba, and Amir is saved from losing Baba's love for setting him up

  • Pg. 164: Soraya asks for Amir's forgiveness for her past

  • Pg. 167: General Taheri forgives Amir at the wedding proceedings, saying “this is the right way—the Afghan way—to do it”.

  • Pg. 192: Rahim Khan and Amir; Amir is seeking forgiveness for his past and he realizes that in order to forgive himself, this is a journey he must take

  • Pg. 210: Hassan forgave his mother and offered his home for her to live and stay

  • Pg. 215-218: Hassan's letters to Amir; ultimate forgiveness that Hassan never held a grudge against Amir

  • Pg. 359: Amir forgives Baba for his actions and realizes that he is no longer holding in the pain from his childhood

  • Pg. 371: Sohrab smiles and talks to Amir during the kite fight — this is his subtle forgiveness

The Cyclic Nature of The Kite Runner

  • Pg. 18: Baba teaching the lesson to Amir that stealing is the ultimate sin; comes full circle later in the book with forgiving Hassan for stealing

  • Pg. 71, 291: Slingshot with Hassan and Sohrab

  • Pg. 92, 289: Assef beating Amir; first time he felt at peace since 1975

  • Pg. 124: Kamal (friend of Assef who helped him rape Hassan) is raped by a Russian soldier on the journey to Pakistan, and dies on the trip. His father commits suicide.

  • Pg. 215-218: Hassan's letters, where Amir realizes his actions as a child could have drastically changed the outcome of Ali, Hassan, and his entire family

  • Pg. 222-225: Rahim Khan reveals that Hassan was Amir's half-brother, and all of Amir's childhood struggles with Baba are partially explained — even though Baba is deceased, this affects Amir and Baba's relationship

  • Pg. 264: Amir revisits the carvings on the tree from his childhood with Hassan

  • Pg. 281: When Amir realizes the Taliban official is Assef, and the past floods in

  • Pg. 125, 320: Amir promises to take Sohrab to America, much like Baba took Amir to America

  • Pg. 67, 371: The final kite fight — how kite fighting continues to represent the relationship between father and son, Baba and Amir, Amir and Hassan, and other relationships (a thousand times over)
