Themes of Angels & Demons

Science v. Religion

Science and religion is one of the most often recurring elements of theme throughout the novel. The book's entire premise is a constant struggle between the forces of religion with the Catholic Church and science, with the most obvious apparition being the antimatter threatening to destroy the city. Throughout the book, these two forces are constantly competing, as the scientific conspiracies of the Illuminati combat the traditional rituals and ceremonies practiced by the Catholic Church. By the end of the novel, we almost reach a truce between science and faith with Cardinal Mortati's voice of reason that the spiritual world and the scientific world can be seamlessly integrated.

Good v. Evil

This theme is present in almost all aspects of the novel, as many of the perceived institutions that would be understood to be “do good” or make a positive impact on the world can be the cause of evil or conspiracy. On the alternative, it also demonstrates how seemingly ordinary or normal people can become courageous and heroic role models when they might not have been expected to do so. We see the struggle between good and evil constantly through the threats of the anti-matter exploding and decimating the Vatican and all who reside in the city. Camerlengo Carlo Ventresca is the best example of a character most affected by this element of theme as all of his actions are a struggle between was is perceived to be good, but is actually a deeper, darker evil force within him.

Morality v. Sin

Morality and sin are two elements of theme that are constantly combating each other, especially within Ventresca. His internal actions he perceives to be righteous and truly moral under the realm of being done in the name of God and faith, when in reality, his actions are forms of severe sin and betrayal. Ventresca is internally convinced that his sins are righteous until the moment of vindication for his crimes by Langdon. This theme also supports the idea that sin, crime, and ill-doing can be deceiving and portrayed as morality and true faith, depending on the medium of which it is conveyed. In the instance of Ventresca, this medium was the message and purpose of God, Christianity, and the Catholic Church, and he was blinded by his own vision of morality that it corrupted his internal differentiation between good will and sin.

Modesty v. Pride

Modesty versus pride is a consistent battle that occurs throughout multiple places in the book. In many cases, several characters let their prideful character control their actions, and they berate any form of opposition to their decisions in subconscious fear of it damaging their reputation or pride. On the other hand, the trait of modesty combats the prideful, irrational behavior of other characters, and this trait is most notably championed by Langdon and Vetra. Another example of this struggle of behavioral traits is within Ventresca's actions, as his actions and the way he wishes to be perceived others is influenced by his prideful and stubborn personality, and had he exemplified a greater sense of modesty, perhaps much of the crisis described in the novel could have been averted.

Public v. Private

The conflicting issues of public versus private is another recurring element of theme in the novel. Throughout the course of the book, many of the executive officials holding a significant amount of knowledge choose to not inform the public or the face of authority, despite the pleas of Langdon and Vetra. The Swiss Guard's decision to not believe this knowledge and then the church hierarchy's decision to withhold this knowledge from the public amount to a potential risk in security and well-being of the people, as had the anti-matter exploded, several lives could have been saved by proper course of action and evacuation. In the end of the conflict of public versus private, we see that public is the dominating force, especially with Ventresca's excruciating confession and suicide in front of the people of the Vatican.
